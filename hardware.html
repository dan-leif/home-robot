Home Robot — Hardware Guide

Budget options for a dedicated always-on "brain" to replace the dev laptop.

Context — what this hardware has to do
Current reference: RTX 4060 8 GB → ~47 tok/s warm on llama3.1:8b Q4_K_M. Feeling real-time requires roughly ≥ 15–20 tok/s so spoken answers start arriving quickly. The two viable paths: (a) a newer AMD Ryzen mini PC whose RDNA 3 iGPU can offload model layers via ROCm, or (b) a mini PC with a PCIe slot for a real discrete GPU. Always-on also means low idle power (~10–20 W) and fanless-quiet during normal use.
The Brain — Compute
Budget
Beelink SER5 Pro / GMKtec K8 Pro
~$280–$380
Ryzen 7 5825U or 7730U
32 GB DDR4 · 500 GB–1 TB NVMe
Radeon Vega 8 iGPU · ~28 W load
~8–12 tok/s on llama3.1:8b (CPU only)
Ollama cannot offload to Vega-generation iGPUs — pure CPU inference. Workable for 3B Qwen models (~18–22 tok/s). Will feel noticeably sluggish for 8B responses. Best treated as a stepping stone; upgrade the box later if speed frustrates you.
★ Recommended
Beelink SER8 / GMKtec NucBox G3 Pro
~$500–$650
Ryzen 7 8845HS
32 GB DDR5-5600 · 1 TB NVMe
Radeon 780M iGPU (12 CUs, RDNA 3) · ~35 W load
~15–25 tok/s on llama3.1:8b (iGPU via ROCm)
Ollama + ROCm offloads model layers to the 780M, sharing the fast DDR5-5600 memory pool (~80 GB/s bandwidth). Warm responses feel snappy. Handles llama3.1:8b today and future Qwen 3–8B models well. Quiet, low idle power, no fuss to set up on Linux.
Premium
Minisforum MS-S1 MAX
~$1,200–$1,600
Ryzen AI Max+ 395 (Strix Halo)
96 GB unified DDR5 · 2 TB SSD
Radeon 8060S (40 CUs, RDNA 3.5) · ~60 W load
~55+ tok/s on 8B · 20–40 tok/s on 70B models
CPU, GPU, and NPU share a single 96 GB memory pool (like Apple Silicon). Runs 70B models that don't fit on any consumer NVIDIA GPU. Massive overkill for this project today — but future-proof for years of model upgrades.
Minisforum MS-A2 + low-profile GPU   ~$1,050–$1,150
Ryzen 9 9955HX barebones ($799) + a low-profile RX 7600 XT or RTX 4060 LP (~$250–$300). The MS-A2's PCIe x16 slot accepts a real discrete GPU — matching or beating the current RTX 4060 laptop. Supports up to 96 GB DDR5. Best option if you want discrete-GPU performance without going full Strix Halo, and leaves room to upgrade the GPU later.
Mac Mini M4 (Apple Silicon)   $599 (16 GB) / $999 M4 Pro (24 GB)
Ollama uses Apple Metal — ~20–30 tok/s on llama3.1:8b. Silent, fanless, excellent watt-for-dollar. The catch: macOS means running Home Assistant Container instead of HA OS (more setup steps), and 16 GB is tight once macOS + HA + the model are all loaded. The M4 Pro at $999 with 24 GB unified memory is comfortable and has faster memory bandwidth. Worth considering if you prefer macOS or already own one.
Memory — RAM
How much RAM
⚠️
16 GB — too tight for a dedicated host
HA OS + faster-whisper + Kokoro TTS + llama3.1:8b pinned in RAM adds up to 12–14 GB active. Leaves almost no headroom; any spike causes swapping and introduces multi-second stalls right when you want fast responses.
✅
32 GB — the right amount for this workload pick this
Fits HA, Whisper, Kokoro, and llama3.1:8b with 6–8 GB to spare. Both recommended mini PCs (SER8, NucBox G3 Pro) ship with 32 GB DDR5 — no upgrade needed. On these systems the RAM is also the GPU's VRAM, so more is always better for layer offloading.
🔮
64–96 GB — unified memory (Strix Halo / Apple Silicon)
On the MS-S1 MAX or Mac Mini, all RAM is GPU-accessible at full bandwidth. 96 GB means the GPU holds a 70B model entirely in one shot — no CPU-offload penalty. On standard DDR5 mini PCs, 64 GB offers no practical benefit unless you're running 30B+ models.
Audio — Microphone & Speaker
🎙️ Microphone
🔵
ReSpeaker USB Mic Array v2.0 pick this
~$65–$70 · Seeed Studio / Amazon
4-mic ring array, XMOS XVF-3000 DSP, hardware acoustic echo cancellation (AEC), 360° far-field pickup. The community standard for Home Assistant voice projects — plug-and-play USB Audio Class, no drivers needed on Linux. Works reliably from across the room even while the speaker is playing.
🟣
ReSpeaker XVF3800 USB upgrade
~$99 · Amazon
Newer than the v2.0 — updated DSP, up to 5 m far-field range. Worth the step up if the robot lives in a large open room or noisy kitchen where the v2.0's 3 m range isn't enough.
⬜
Blue Snowball iCE budget / near-field
~$40
Single cardioid capsule, no array, no hardware AEC. Fine if the box sits on your desk and you speak within 1–2 m. Will pick up the speaker unless you enable software echo cancellation in the Whisper add-on settings.
📱
HA Voice Preview Edition satellite (end-state) future
~$65 estimated
A self-contained wake-word device: mic array + speaker + LED ring + local wake word, talking to the mini PC brain over the network. Once you have one of these, the standalone mic and speaker become unnecessary — it's all in one box.
🔊 Speaker
🟢
Any compact Bluetooth or 3.5mm speaker
~$20–$35 · e.g. Anker Soundcore Select 4 Go
The assistant's audio plays through whatever is set as the default output on the mini PC. No smart-speaker intelligence needed — it just needs to sound decent from a few feet away. Bluetooth works fine; 3.5mm avoids any pairing hassle.
🖥️
Powered desktop speaker you already own
$0
Any USB or 3.5mm powered speaker works. Don't buy one until the mic + AEC setup is confirmed working — the speaker you buy last week might already be sitting on your desk.

Cheaper alternative
GMKtec NucBox K8 Pro or Beelink SER5 Pro + ReSpeaker mic — ~$350–$450 total.

Drop to Ryzen 7730U and CPU-only inference. Fine if you switch to a 3B Qwen model (strong Chinese, fast on CPU, ~18–22 tok/s). 8B models will feel slow (~10 tok/s). Upgrade the box later if speed frustrates you.
Nicer alternative
Minisforum MS-A2 barebones + low-profile RX 7600 XT — ~$1,050–$1,150 total.

Real discrete GPU in a mini PC. Matches the current RTX 4060 laptop at warm inference. Upgradeable to 96 GB DDR5 for bigger models later. Best long-term investment if this becomes a permanent home server.
Why the cheap eBay Intel mini PCs don't work here: 7th-gen Kaby Lake i5s (OptiPlex 3050, EliteDesk 800 G3 — circa 2016–2017) land at ~3–8 tok/s on 8B models, have no ROCm-compatible iGPU, and run narrow DDR4-2400 at about 38 GB/s memory bandwidth — roughly half what a modern DDR5 Ryzen has. At $180–$200 they look like a bargain, but they can't run this workload at real-time speeds. They'd be fine hosting just the HA OS part, but not the Ollama brain.
